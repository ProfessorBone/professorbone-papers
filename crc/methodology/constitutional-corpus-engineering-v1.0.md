# Abstract

Technical research often begins as a sequence of papers. As the sequence grows, thematic similarity alone does not make it a coherent corpus. Definitions can drift, authority can become ambiguous, schemas can fork, versions can obscure active state, and later papers can accidentally acquire power over concepts they only meant to explain. Constitutional Corpus Engineering, or CCE, names a method for treating this problem as an engineering problem rather than a filing problem.

The central claim is simple: A coherent technical corpus is not merely written. It is engineered as a governed knowledge system. The CCE method proposes that a corpus becomes governable when its artifacts declare what they own, what they consume, what they elaborate, what they specialize, what they cannot redefine, and how their versions, dependencies, findings, registries, navigation surfaces, and publication gates interact.

This paper uses the Constitutional Runtime Computation corpus as its originating case study. CRC provides a live example of corpus engineering through [Core v5.14](../constitutional-runtime-computation-v5.14.md), [CRC-CORPUS-ENTRY](../corpus-entry/README.md), [CRC-SSR v1.6](../shared-schema-registry-v1.6.md), [CRC-OAR v1.4](../open-architecture-register-v1.4.md), the active [CRC README](../README.md), and the companion papers that reconciled standing, actuation, task state, tools, and BindingRecord lineage. CRC is evidence for the method, not a universal template for every corpus.

The paper is explanatory and methodological. It does not alter CRC runtime doctrine, define agent-runtime behavior, change CRC-OAR lifecycle status, introduce a shared schema, or amend CRC-CORPUS-ENTRY. Its contribution is to name, synthesize, structure, explain, and generalize the corpus-engineering practice demonstrated by CRC. Its limits are equally important: the method originates from one corpus case study, requires external validation, and does not present CRC's exact artifact classes, registries, gates, or lifecycle vocabulary as requirements for other technical corpora.

# Introduction

Technical research rarely grows in a straight line. A parent paper defines the first architecture. A companion paper fills one gap. A later paper specializes a surface the parent left open. A registry is introduced so shared terms can be found. An architecture register is added so unresolved questions can be seen instead of silently carried. A README helps readers navigate the active state. Over time, the repository becomes more than a folder of documents, but it may not yet be a coherent corpus.

The difficulty is not only volume. A small corpus can be incoherent if two artifacts claim the same definition, if a registry repeats schema content instead of pointing to its canonical owner, if navigation language is read as doctrine, or if a draft enters the repository without declaring whether it defines, consumes, elaborates, or specializes existing concepts. Ordinary file organization can place related documents near each other, but it does not answer authority questions.

CCE is derived from the CRC experience. In CRC, the active corpus includes the parent doctrine, companion papers, a shared-schema registry, an open architecture register, a corpus-entry process, navigation, and version history. Those surfaces are not interchangeable. [Core v5.14](../constitutional-runtime-computation-v5.14.md) defines runtime doctrine and canonical shared apparatus where Core owns it. [CRC-SSR v1.6](../shared-schema-registry-v1.6.md) records canonical locations and operative versions by pointer. [CRC-OAR v1.4](../open-architecture-register-v1.4.md) records architecture-finding status and closure history. [CRC-CORPUS-ENTRY](../corpus-entry/README.md) governs admission of artifacts. The [CRC README](../README.md) navigates the active corpus without becoming defining doctrine.

The CCE method generalizes from that arrangement. It asks how a distributed technical literature can be made legible as a governed knowledge architecture. In this title, constitutional refers to governed authority relationships derived from the CRC case, not to constitutional law and not to a new layer of CRC runtime doctrine. The method requires further external validation. It does not claim that CRC's exact structures are mandatory elsewhere. It claims that the underlying engineering problem recurs wherever a body of technical artifacts must preserve coherent ownership, dependency, schema, lifecycle, version, and publication state.

# From Document Collection to Governed Corpus

A stack of papers is a set of documents. It may have order, but the order need not define authority. A document collection is more organized: papers may be grouped by topic, date, author, or project. An indexed repository improves discovery by listing files, titles, and perhaps versions. None of these states by itself creates a governed corpus.

A governed technical corpus is a body of artifacts whose relationships are explicit with respect to canonical ownership, authority boundaries, dependency direction, schema location, artifact classification, lifecycle state, version history, admission, publication, correction, and supersession. The key difference is not polish. The key difference is that the corpus can answer governance questions that ordinary organization leaves implicit.

In the CCE method, the corpus does not need every artifact to define everything it discusses. A paper can define a concept, elaborate a concept owned elsewhere, specialize a generic object within a declared scope, consume a registry pointer, cite a historical state, or provide navigation. These are different relationships. Coherence improves when they are separated.

These categories are prose methodology concepts in this paper. They are not shared schemas, predicates, registry objects, or CRC artifact classes. Any future attempt to formalize a CCE term as a shared object would require its own governed proposal and registry treatment.

# Corpus as a Governed Knowledge System

The CCE method proposes that a corpus can be understood as a governed knowledge system. Storage is necessary, but storage is not sufficient. A corpus also needs rules or conventions that determine how artifacts interact.

A governed corpus can include foundational doctrine, companion or elaborating artifacts, schemas, registries, architecture findings, implementation specifications, navigation surfaces, validation artifacts, and historical versions. These surfaces have different jobs. Foundational doctrine can define the generic architecture. Companions can elaborate or specialize it. Registries can record where shared objects are canonically defined. Architecture findings can make incompleteness visible. Navigation can show readers which artifacts are active. Historical versions can preserve the path by which the corpus reached its current state.

In CRC, these roles are instantiated by specific artifacts. Core v5.14 is the active parent doctrine. The companion sequence includes papers such as [Constitutional Standing v1.7](../companions/constitutional-standing-v1.7.md), [Constitutional Actuation and Effect Admission v1.0](../companions/constitutional-actuation-and-effect-admission-v1.0.md), [Constitutional Tools v1.2](../companions/constitutional-tools-v1.2.md), and [Constitutional Task Ledger v1.4](../companions/constitutional-task-ledger-v1.4.md). CRC-SSR and CRC-OAR perform registry and architecture-finding roles. CRC-CORPUS-ENTRY governs entry and publication. The README remains a navigation surface.

The CRC case demonstrates one implementation of a broader method. A different corpus can choose different names, statuses, file locations, or artifact classes. The CCE claim is that the relationships themselves need governance if the corpus is to remain coherent under growth.

# Canonical Ownership

Canonical ownership is a corpus-coherence mechanism. It answers the question: where does the generic definition of this concept live? Without that answer, later papers can accidentally create multiple definitions of one object, each plausible in its local context and each incompatible with the others.

The CCE method distinguishes definition, elaboration, specialization, consumption, historical reference, and navigation. A defining artifact owns the generic concept. An elaborating artifact explains consequences without changing the generic definition. A specializing artifact applies the concept within a narrower declared scope. A consuming artifact relies on the concept without owning it. A historical reference points to a prior state. A navigation surface helps readers find the active source but does not itself become that source.

CRC provides clear examples. Core Appendix B in [Core v5.14](../constitutional-runtime-computation-v5.14.md) establishes active shared apparatus and related ownership settlements. [CRC-SSR v1.6](../shared-schema-registry-v1.6.md) records canonical locations and operative versions by pointer rather than reproducing schemas. [CRC-CORPUS-ENTRY](../corpus-entry/README.md) requires proposed artifacts to declare authority and dependency relationships before admission.

Multiple artifacts may discuss one concept while only one artifact owns its generic definition. In the F-3 reconciliation, Standing discusses BindingRecord because standing evidence can be consumed by BindingRecord. Task Ledger discusses BindingRecord because CycleRecord Bind outcome lineage can point to it. C10 discusses BindingRecord because actuation and effect admission create upstream dependency requirements. Core owns the canonical BindingRecord schema and BindingRecordValid predicate.

# Authority Boundaries

Ownership must be paired with boundaries. A corpus can know where a definition lives and still drift if artifacts silently expand their authority beyond their declared scope.

The CCE method treats authority expansion as a central failure mode. A companion can overreach by restating a parent predicate as if it can alter it. A specialization can become a silent redefinition if it removes required generic constraints. A registry can become an accidental schema owner if it copies schema text instead of pointing to the defining artifact. A README can become accidental doctrine if its summaries are treated as normative. An architecture register can become a solution owner if it is read as defining the resolution rather than tracking the finding's lifecycle.

CRC addresses these risks by separating roles. [CRC-SSR v1.6](../shared-schema-registry-v1.6.md) records pointer authority, not schema text. [CRC-OAR v1.4](../open-architecture-register-v1.4.md) records architecture-finding status and closure history, not runtime doctrine. [CRC-CORPUS-ENTRY](../corpus-entry/README.md) governs admission and publication process, not runtime behavior. The [CRC README](../README.md) helps readers navigate active artifacts, but it does not override the artifacts it lists.

This paper consumes those roles as evidence. It does not own them. CCE explains why such boundaries matter, while CRC's live artifacts remain the authority for CRC's actual rules.

# Shared-Schema Governance

Shared objects need stable names and owners because they are consumed across artifact boundaries. If an object is used by Core, companions, registries, and implementation specifications, a reader must be able to locate its canonical definition and know which version is operative.

The CCE method separates definition from registration. A defining artifact states the schema or predicate. A registry records the canonical location and operative version. Consumers cite the defining source or registry pointer and remain bound to the canonical object. Specializers may add scope-specific constraints only where the corpus permits specialization and where they preserve the generic requirements.

In CRC, [CRC-SSR v1.6](../shared-schema-registry-v1.6.md) is the active shared-schema registry. It records canonical locations and operative versions by pointer. It does not hold schema text. Core Appendix B in [Core v5.14](../constitutional-runtime-computation-v5.14.md) establishes the registry role and related shared-apparatus settlements.

Prose terminology does not automatically become a shared schema. This paper uses terms such as governed technical corpus, corpus authority mapping, corpus coherence, and coordinated corpus publication package as explanatory methodology terms. It does not register them in CRC-SSR, define canonical schema fields for them, or require CRC consumers to treat them as governed objects.

# Architecture Findings and Lifecycle Governance

Architecture findings make corpus incompleteness visible. They identify questions the corpus has not yet settled, track work as it moves through specification, and preserve the difference between a proposed answer, a published specification, and a formally resolved finding.

In CRC, the active lifecycle vocabulary preserved by [CRC-OAR v1.4](../open-architecture-register-v1.4.md) is:

OPEN
-> SPECIFYING
-> SPECIFIED
-> RESOLVED

The difference matters. OPEN identifies a question. SPECIFYING indicates active specification work. SPECIFIED indicates that the necessary specification has been published. RESOLVED indicates that the finding has satisfied its closure conditions and has been formally advanced. Publication alone does not equal closure, because a published artifact can still leave registry settlement, consumer alignment, navigation, or historical closure unresolved.

This lifecycle is CRC's active case-study mechanism. The CCE method proposes the more general idea that unresolved corpus architecture should be represented visibly and advanced deliberately. It does not require every corpus to use the same status names or transitions.

# Closure Conditions

Closure should be finding-specific. A finding about a missing schema owner may close under different evidence than a finding about public artifact admission or lifecycle ambiguity.

The CRC case suggests that closure evidence can include canonical ownership, schema publication, predicate definition, registry treatment, consumer alignment, specialization alignment, dependency settlement, active navigation, historical preservation, public publication, and explicit human ratification. These are examples of evidence categories, not a new CRC rule.

The CCE method treats closure as a governance claim. A closure claim says not only that something was written, but that the relevant dependent surfaces have been reconciled. A finding can be specified without being resolved. A paper can be published without all consumers being aligned. A registry can point to a canonical owner while a navigation surface still lags. Closure is strongest when these relationships are checked rather than assumed.

In CRC, [CRC-OAR v1.4](../open-architecture-register-v1.4.md) records the final lifecycle state of the F-3 and F-5 workstream. This paper cites that state as historical and governance evidence. It does not change it.

# Coordinated Publication Packages

Corpus-level changes often require coordinated multi-artifact packages. A definition may change in one artifact, but consumers, registries, architecture findings, and navigation may also need alignment. If only the defining artifact changes, the corpus can remain inconsistent even after the central specification is correct.

The CCE method treats a publication package as the bounded set of files needed to make one governed change coherent. That package can include defining artifacts, elaborating artifacts, consuming artifacts, registries, architecture registers, navigation, and historical records. It should also identify protected surfaces that must not change without separate authorization.

Partial reconciliation is dangerous because it creates local correctness with corpus-level ambiguity. A schema can be defined but not registered. A registry can be updated while consumers still refer to an old owner. An architecture finding can be marked resolved before its dependent artifacts have been published. A README can point to a draft as if it were active. These are not merely editorial errors; they are authority errors.

The F-3 and F-5 workstream provides CRC evidence for this principle. The detailed case-study section later in this paper discusses how BindingRecord reconciliation, actuation and effect admission, registry treatment, consumer alignment, and OAR lifecycle closure interacted.

# Constitutional Publication Gates

Publication gates answer different governance questions. They are not only procedural friction. They separate analysis, authorization, drafting, review, application, staging, committing, pushing, and post-publication verification so that each step can be evaluated on its own terms.

The CCE method explains the following gate types as an abstraction from CRC practice. Read-only analysis asks what the corpus already authorizes. A proposal asks whether the artifact has a lawful class, scope, path, authority boundary, and dependency treatment. Draft authorization asks whether a text may be written. Coherence review asks whether the draft contradicts active doctrine or misstates ownership. Hygiene review asks whether the draft satisfies mechanical and publication-quality constraints. Ratification asks whether a human authority accepts the artifact or package.

Authorized apply asks whether repository files may be changed. Unstaged review asks what the working tree actually contains before staging. Exact-path staging asks whether only the authorized files enter the staged set. Staged review asks whether the committed candidate still matches the authorized package. Commit authorization asks whether the staged state may become history. Push authorization asks whether local history may become remote-public. Post-publication verification asks whether active navigation, repository status, and publication state match the intended result.

This is explanatory synthesis of CRC practice, especially [CRC-CORPUS-ENTRY](../corpus-entry/README.md) and the review workflow in [09-review-and-publication-workflow.md](../corpus-entry/09-review-and-publication-workflow.md). It does not copy the process manual and does not amend CRC's gates.

# Exact-Path Change Control

Package boundaries should identify exact files. A governed corpus can lose coherence when a change set includes nearby edits that were never authorized, or when a broad staging command captures unrelated work.

Exact-path change control supports auditability. It lets reviewers compare the authorized package to the actual working tree, then compare the actual working tree to the staged candidate. The staged diff becomes a distinct object of review because it is the state that would become commit history if authorized.

Protected surfaces matter for the same reason. A methodology paper may be authorized while Core, CRC-OAR, CRC-SSR, CRC-CORPUS-ENTRY, README, and companions remain protected. If the draft discovers a real need to alter one of those surfaces, the correct response is not to include the edit opportunistically. The correct response is to stop and route the new need through its own authorization path.

CCE treats exact-path control as a practical way to prevent scope expansion. It makes the difference between writing an artifact and changing the corpus.

# Historical Preservation

Historical versions are governance evidence. They show what the corpus previously said, when ambiguities existed, how dependencies were understood, which lifecycle transitions occurred, and how corrections or supersessions were made.

Coherence should not be achieved by erasing the path through which the corpus evolved. If prior versions are silently replaced, the corpus loses the ability to explain why a later settlement mattered. Former ambiguity can be important evidence. A resolved finding can only be understood as resolved if the prior unresolved state remains traceable.

The CRC case uses versioned artifacts and historical version histories throughout the active corpus. [CRC-OAR v1.4](../open-architecture-register-v1.4.md) also preserves architecture-finding history, including the F-3 and F-5 lifecycle state. The CCE method generalizes this as historical coherence: the current active state should be legible without making prior states disappear.

# Navigation as a Governed Surface

Navigation is a governed surface because it shapes how readers encounter authority. It identifies active artifacts, historical artifacts, companion relationships, governance artifacts, and recommended reading order. Navigation can reduce confusion, but it can also create confusion if it overstates authority.

The CCE method distinguishes active navigation, historical navigation, version identification, artifact classification, and relationship mapping. A navigation file can say which artifact is active and where it is located. It can describe the artifact's role. It can link related governance surfaces. It should not become the source of authority for the concepts it references.

In CRC, the [CRC README](../README.md) is navigation evidence. It points readers to active Core, companion papers, CRC-SSR, CRC-OAR, and CRC-CORPUS-ENTRY. It does not define BindingRecord, ActuationCommand, lifecycle status, artifact classes, or admission rules. Those definitions remain in their canonical artifacts.

# CRC Case Study

CRC is the originating case study for CCE. The case is useful because it shows a corpus moving from a set of related technical papers toward a governed architecture with explicit ownership, registry treatment, architecture-finding status, publication gates, and active navigation.

The case should not be overstated. CRC's local conventions are not universal requirements. CRC's artifact classes, status names, companion sequence, and exact admission process are CRC-specific. The generalizable lesson is that corpus coherence requires explicit relationships among artifacts.

## Initial Corpus Condition

The CRC repository grew as a set of intellectually related papers. The parent doctrine established the constitutional runtime architecture. Companions explored boundary contracts, memory, retrieval, baselines, coherence, thresholds, standing, tools, task state, and actuation. As the corpus grew, several kinds of uncertainty became visible: ownership ambiguity, schema uncertainty, implicit dependencies, unresolved architecture findings, version and navigation concerns, and publication-state questions.

This does not mean the papers were invalid or incoherent in their local claims. It means the corpus had reached a scale at which local correctness was not enough. Concepts such as BindingRecord, ContinuationState, CycleRecord, ToolResultRecord, ActuationResult, and TaskLedger interacted across paper boundaries. A coherent corpus required a governed account of those interactions.

## Corpus Governance Apparatus

CRC's governance apparatus includes several surfaces with distinct authority. [Core v5.14](../constitutional-runtime-computation-v5.14.md) owns active parent doctrine and canonical shared apparatus where Core defines it. Core Appendix B records active shared-apparatus settlements. [CRC-SSR v1.6](../shared-schema-registry-v1.6.md) records canonical locations and operative versions by pointer. [CRC-OAR v1.4](../open-architecture-register-v1.4.md) records architecture findings and their lifecycle state. [CRC-CORPUS-ENTRY](../corpus-entry/README.md) governs corpus admission and publication. The [CRC README](../README.md) provides navigation.

Companion ownership boundaries are equally important. [Constitutional Standing v1.7](../companions/constitutional-standing-v1.7.md) owns standing-specific doctrine and contributes standing applicability and non-applicability evidence to BindingRecord. [Constitutional Task Ledger v1.4](../companions/constitutional-task-ledger-v1.4.md) owns TaskLedger, PendingObligation, CycleRecord, and related task-state structures. [Constitutional Actuation and Effect Admission v1.0](../companions/constitutional-actuation-and-effect-admission-v1.0.md) supplies actuation and effect-admission branch mechanics without owning final BindingRecord reconciliation.

The governance apparatus did not make every artifact the same kind of source. It made their differences explicit.

## BindingRecord Reconciliation

The F-3 ambiguity concerned BindingRecord ownership and lineage. Several artifacts needed to discuss binding because binding sits at the point where a favorable constitutional outcome acquires causal force over governed state. The corpus needed one canonical owner for the generic BindingRecord schema and BindingRecordValid predicate, plus clear consumer boundaries for Standing, Task Ledger, actuation, and tools.

In the reconciled CRC state, [Core v5.14](../constitutional-runtime-computation-v5.14.md) owns BindingRecord and BindingRecordValid. Binding occurs at valid applied GovernedStateMutationRecord. BindingRecord is evidentiary rather than constitutive: it records and proves a binding event, but it does not create the binding event. A completed BindingRecord must not precede the governed mutation it records.

[Constitutional Standing v1.7](../companions/constitutional-standing-v1.7.md) contributes standing applicability, positive standing non-applicability evidence, standing validation evidence, StandingFailureRecord, and timing or revalidation doctrine. It does not own the generic BindingRecord schema or generic BindingRecordValid. Core consumes only the completed standing dispositions APPLICABLE_VALID and NOT_APPLICABLE for BindingRecord validity, while missing, failed, stale, unavailable, or invalid required standing evidence is a non-bind condition.

[Constitutional Task Ledger v1.4](../companions/constitutional-task-ledger-v1.4.md) repairs CycleRecord Bind outcome lineage by requiring a Bind outcome to use a BindingRecord reference. It preserves Task Ledger ownership of CycleRecord and PendingObligation while consuming Core-owned BindingRecord. [CRC-SSR v1.6](../shared-schema-registry-v1.6.md) records the Core ownership settlement for BindingRecord. [CRC-OAR v1.4](../open-architecture-register-v1.4.md) records OAR-F003 as RESOLVED.

This reconciliation illustrates CCE's ownership principle. The corpus did not need every artifact to stop discussing BindingRecord. It needed each artifact to state the mode of that discussion.

## Actuation and Effect Admission

F-5 addressed the missing generic layer between adjudicative authorization and governed effect. Before the F-5 workstream, the corpus separated proposal from Resolution and Tools separated invocation authorization from result admission, but the generic constitutional chain from Resolution to effect, result, admission, mutation, binding, cycle recording, Task Ledger update, ContinuationState, and AgentObservation needed explicit treatment.

The active CRC chain can be summarized as:

Resolution
-> ActuationCommand where applicable
-> attempt
-> ActuationResult where applicable
-> ActuationAdmissionRecord
-> GovernedStateMutationRecord
-> BindingRecord
-> CycleRecord
-> Task Ledger update
-> ContinuationState
-> AgentObservation

This sequence distinguishes authorization, attempt, result, admission, mutation, binding evidence, cycle closure, and continuation. Authorization does not equal execution. Execution does not equal admission. Admission does not equal mutation. Mutation does not equal BindingRecord. Binding evidence does not equal task closure. Continuation is issued by the substrate, not privately carried by the agent.

[Core v5.14](../constitutional-runtime-computation-v5.14.md) owns canonical F-5 objects including ActuationCommand, ActuationResult, ActuationAdmissionRecord, and GovernedStateMutationRecord, alongside the Core-owned BindingRecord settlement. [Constitutional Actuation and Effect Admission v1.0](../companions/constitutional-actuation-and-effect-admission-v1.0.md) supplies branch mechanics, failure topology, retry and idempotency doctrine, compensation treatment, audit reconstruction requirements, and conformance rules for specializers.

The F-5 apparatus addresses internal and external branches, partial effects, unknown results, retry, idempotency, duplicate effects, rollback, compensation, and audit durability. [Constitutional Tools v1.2](../companions/constitutional-tools-v1.2.md) aligns tool invocation and result admission with the generic F-5 chain: ToolInvocationProposal remains a proposal, a substrate tool actuator derives a tool-specific command conforming to ActuationCommand, tool execution is an actuation attempt, ToolResultRecord specializes ActuationResult, ToolResultAdmissible specializes result admission, and downstream state effects conform to GovernedStateMutationRecord.

This paper does not redefine those objects or predicates. It uses them as evidence that corpus coherence required separating generic actuation ownership, branch mechanics, specializer alignment, registry treatment, and lifecycle closure.

## Lifecycle Closure

[CRC-OAR v1.4](../open-architecture-register-v1.4.md) records the final lifecycle state for the F-3 and F-5 workstream. The sequencing matters. CRC-OAR v1.3 moved OAR-F003 from SPECIFYING to SPECIFIED, moved OAR-F005 from SPECIFIED to RESOLVED, and moved the F-5 apparatus entries from SPECIFYING to SPECIFIED. OAR-F005 was the only newly RESOLVED entry in v1.3. CRC-OAR v1.4 then moved exactly ten entries from SPECIFIED to RESOLVED: OAR-F003 plus OAR-A001, OAR-A002, OAR-P001, OAR-A003, and OAR-X001 through OAR-X005. OAR-F005 remained RESOLVED from v1.3.

The active register shows no OPEN, BLOCKED, SPECIFYING, or SPECIFIED remainder for that workstream. This is a lifecycle claim about the F-3 and F-5 workstream, not a claim that every possible CRC architecture question is closed.

F-5 could be specified and published before F-3 was complete because C10 supplied a dependency contract without resolving BindingRecord ownership. F-3 later reconciled BindingRecord through Core v5.14, Standing v1.7, Task Ledger v1.4, CRC-SSR v1.6, and OAR lifecycle advancement. The final OAR state records the workstream as lifecycle-closed.

Lifecycle closure did not itself change doctrine. The doctrine changed through the defining artifacts and their admitted versions. CRC-OAR recorded the finding state. This distinction is central to CCE: registers track governance state, while defining artifacts own definitions.

# Corpus Coherence as an Engineering Property

The paper's theoretical contribution is to treat corpus coherence as an engineering property. Coherence is not uniform prose, a single authorial voice, or a directory structure. It arises from governed relationships among artifacts.

A coherent corpus should provide reliable answers about ownership, definition, dependency, specialization, lifecycle state, active version, historical state, admission, and correction. A reader should be able to ask where a concept is defined, which artifacts consume it, whether a later paper specializes it, which version is active, whether an architecture question remains open, and what process admits new artifacts.

CCE distinguishes several kinds of coherence. Semantic coherence concerns whether terms are used compatibly. Authority coherence concerns whether ownership and redefinition boundaries are clear. Schema coherence concerns whether shared objects have stable names and canonical locations. Lifecycle coherence concerns whether unresolved questions and closure states are visible. Navigational coherence concerns whether readers can find the active state. Historical coherence concerns whether prior states remain traceable.

These are explanatory categories, not registered CRC schemas. They are useful because they let reviewers diagnose different failure modes rather than treating all inconsistency as ordinary editorial cleanup.

# Failure Modes Prevented

The CCE method is designed to reduce several corpus failure modes. It does not claim empirical elimination.

Definition drift occurs when terms evolve differently across artifacts without a canonical owner. Ownership collision occurs when more than one artifact claims the same generic definition. Schema forking occurs when shared objects are copied or modified in local contexts. Specialization escape occurs when a narrow specializer silently weakens or replaces a generic rule. Hidden dependency occurs when an artifact depends on a concept without declaring the dependency.

Premature closure occurs when a finding is treated as resolved before dependent surfaces are reconciled. Historical erasure occurs when prior ambiguity is removed from view, making later settlement impossible to audit. Navigation staleness occurs when the active map points to obsolete or draft artifacts. Informal admission occurs when a draft gains authority because it exists in the repository rather than because it passed the governed entry process. Corpus fragmentation occurs when artifacts remain intellectually related but governance relationships are no longer recoverable.

The CRC case suggests that visible ownership, registry treatment, architecture findings, exact publication packages, and staged publication gates can make these failures easier to detect and harder to normalize.

# General Corpus-Engineering Workflow

The CCE methodology can be stated as a concise workflow:

1. Inventory the artifacts and identify active, draft, historical, governance, registry, navigational, and evidence surfaces.
2. Map authority by determining which artifacts define, elaborate, specialize, consume, or navigate each major concept.
3. Detect conflicts, including ownership collisions, schema forks, version drift, hidden dependencies, and stale navigation.
4. Review shared objects and determine which concepts require canonical location, registry treatment, or no schema treatment.
5. Record unresolved architecture questions in the corpus's chosen finding or issue mechanism where ownership, lifecycle, dependency, or closure remains unsettled.
6. Specify the required defining, consuming, registry, navigation, and historical changes.
7. Publish coordinated packages that include the exact files needed for corpus coherence.
8. Close findings independently only after the closure evidence for each finding is satisfied.
9. Preserve historical versions and state transitions as governance evidence.
10. Maintain active navigation so readers can find the current corpus state.
11. Govern later artifact admission through declared class, scope, authority, dependency, registry, OAR, navigation, versioning, and validation treatment.

This workflow is the CCE methodology. It is not an amendment to CRC-CORPUS-ENTRY. CRC's entry process remains governed by [CRC-CORPUS-ENTRY](../corpus-entry/README.md) and its active guide files.

# AI-Assisted Corpus Engineering

AI assistance can be useful in corpus engineering because the work is comparison-heavy. An AI system can help inventory artifacts, compare terminology, detect possible contradictions, map dependencies, extract candidate schema references, prepare source maps, review consistency, and validate changed-file boundaries.

AI analysis does not create corpus authority. AI generation does not admit an artifact. AI validation does not replace ratification. AI assistance does not authorize autonomous mutation of the corpus. Classification, canonical ownership, doctrinal interpretation, lifecycle advancement, admission, ratification, staging authorization, commit authorization, and publication authorization remain human-governed decisions.

In CRC terms, ORSR may provide an illustrative parallel because it distinguishes agent proposal from substrate-governed authority. This paper does not claim that AI-assisted writing workflow is itself a CRC runtime implementation. The analogy is limited: the method preserves a separation between assistance and authority.

The CCE method can use AI as an analytical instrument while keeping authority external to the instrument. That boundary is necessary because corpus engineering is partly technical and partly constitutional in the ordinary governance sense: deciding what a corpus means cannot be delegated to a tool merely because the tool can summarize the corpus.

# Corpus Entry Governance

Coherence must be maintained after initial reconciliation. A corpus that is made coherent once can fragment again if later artifacts enter without declaring their authority relationships.

The CCE method proposes that new artifacts should declare their purpose, class, authority, concepts defined, concepts consumed, concepts elaborated, dependencies, architecture-register implications, schema-registry implications, navigation effects, versioning, validation requirements, and publication status. These declarations help prevent a draft from acquiring authority merely by existing.

CRC instantiates this principle through [CRC-CORPUS-ENTRY](../corpus-entry/README.md). New papers and public artifacts route through proposal, dependency, authority, registry, OAR, README, validation, and publication review as applicable. This paper describes that process as a case-study component. It does not govern it, amend it, or reinterpret its gates.

# Correction, Reopening, Supersession, and Withdrawal

Resolution does not imply infallibility. A governed corpus should support correction, reopening, supersession, withdrawal, deprecation, and replacement. The exact mechanisms can differ across corpora.

Correction addresses an error without necessarily changing the artifact's role. Reopening addresses a finding or question that was believed settled but has become unsettled under new evidence. Supersession identifies a later artifact or version as the active source in place of an earlier one. Withdrawal removes an artifact from active authority. Deprecation marks an artifact or concept as retained for history but no longer recommended for use. Replacement identifies the new active surface that should be used instead.

CRC's version histories, active navigation, and OAR history provide evidence for this general principle. They show that a corpus can preserve prior states while still making the current active state clear.

# Evaluation Criteria

CCE evaluates a corpus through questions rather than formal predicates. Authority: can the corpus identify which artifact owns each major concept? Schema: can shared objects be found at their canonical location and operative version? Boundaries: can artifacts state what they do not own or cannot redefine? Dependencies: can consumers and specializers identify what they rely on? Findings: are unresolved architecture questions visible?

Lifecycle: can the corpus distinguish open questions, active specification, published specification, and formal resolution? History: are prior versions and former ambiguity preserved? Navigation: can readers find the active state without treating navigation as doctrine? Publication: are change packages bounded and reviewed before public activation? Admission: do new artifacts enter through a governed route? Closure: does each resolved finding have evidence appropriate to that finding?

These are CCE evaluation questions. They are not CRC predicates, shared schemas, or OAR statuses.

# Novelty and Limitations

This paper's novelty is to name, synthesize, structure, explain, and generalize the corpus-engineering method demonstrated through CRC. It frames corpus coherence as an engineering property produced by governed relationships among artifacts, including canonical ownership, authority boundaries, shared-schema governance, architecture findings, publication gates, historical preservation, navigation, and governed admission. It does not claim to invent registries, version control, document governance, architecture decision records, corpus linguistics, constitutional law, or the underlying CRC doctrines.

The central claim is that a coherent technical corpus is not merely written. It is engineered as a governed knowledge system.

The non-claim is equally important: CCE does not treat CRC's exact companion structure, OAR lifecycle vocabulary, SSR pointer registry, file naming pattern, admission gate sequence, or doctrinal architecture as requirements for other technical corpora. Those are CRC-specific implementations.

The method originates from one corpus case study. External validation remains limited. Other corpora may require different artifact classes, registers, schemas, gates, navigation conventions, and publication practices. This paper does not establish empirical superiority over other corpus-governance methods. It does not define runtime behavior. It does not change CRC governance.

# Conclusion

A coherent technical corpus is not merely written. It is engineered as a governed knowledge system.

The transformation from papers to corpus depends on making relationships explicit and governable. A paper can be excellent in isolation and still leave the corpus with unresolved ownership, hidden dependencies, schema ambiguity, stale navigation, or premature closure. CCE treats those problems as engineering problems because they can be surfaced, bounded, reviewed, and governed.

The CRC case demonstrates one way to perform that transformation. Core, companions, CRC-SSR, CRC-OAR, CRC-CORPUS-ENTRY, README navigation, version history, and publication gates together form a knowledge architecture in which artifacts are not merely adjacent. They are related by explicit authority.

In a governed corpus, each artifact should know what it owns, what it consumes, what it may elaborate, what it may specialize, what it may not redefine, what it depends on, and where it stands in the lifecycle of the whole.
